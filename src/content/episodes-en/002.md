The king died the night before his coronation.

More precisely, the king's body died. His heart stopped, his breath ended, and his fingertips cooled like wax. Three royal physicians confirmed the death in three different ways. One held a silver mirror before his mouth. One took the pulse at his throat. The last pressed an ear to the royal chest and remained there a long time.

They reached the same conclusion.

<span class="memory-interference">The king was no longer alive.</span>

The court did not prepare a funeral.

The coronation in the new capital was not canceled. More incense was sent to the cathedral, more soldiers were placed around the square, and more proclamations were fixed to walls. Nowhere did the words the king is dead appear. Instead, the papers repeated one phrase: the king's return.

Ian read it and laughed for longer than was wise.

The laughter stopped quickly. He had no right to it. For three days after the first collection, he had written more sentences into Krontium than anyone in the city. The market paid when it opened. The bridge paid when it received its first procession. The prison paid when it swallowed its first prisoner. Ian had stood before every record.

He was now a man with a scentless kitchen.

He thought he could endure that. People survived more loss than they expected. Even without the smell of soup, hunger returned, and his mother's face remained. But what vanished afterward was not only scent. One morning he could not remember whether his first teacher had been left-handed. The next day, the color of his childhood door disappeared. The losses were always small, which made them cruel.

A great loss drives a person to the knees.

A small loss lets him keep walking.

On the coronation day, the cathedral looked like the city's heart. Black Krontium ribs held the ceiling, and panes of gold glass filled the spaces between them. Sunlight passed through and drew a crown-shaped shadow on the floor. People avoided stepping on it. No one had announced such a rule. Everyone knew it anyway.

The king's corpse entered through the rear door wrapped in white cloth.

Officially, he was recovering from illness. Officially, he had chosen silence to receive the blessing of the new capital. Officially, nothing had happened.

Ian disliked the word officially.

The crown rested in the center of the cathedral. It was darker than gold, deeper than iron, older-looking than bone. The Krontium crown was not an ornament. Tiny thorns lined its interior. When it touched a king's head, the thorns pierced skin, touched skull, and found the roads of memory beneath.

“Will it bring him back?”

Ian asked.

Maret looked toward the doors before answering. People were pouring in: nobles in black, commoners in their cleanest clothes. They had come to witness a miracle. Miracles made people docile. Few were angry in the presence of one.

“It will postpone death,” Maret said.

“For how long?”

“For as long as the kingdom pays.”

Ian hated the answer. It left all the doors open. Who was the kingdom? The bloodline, the wall, the people in the square, the taxpayers, the collector?

He already knew.

<span class="memory-interference">Krontium omitted no one.</span>

The bell rang.

When the cathedral doors closed, silence fell even outside. The crowd could not see in, but Krontium lines in the floor connected interior and square. A miracle did not happen in a sealed room. A miracle passed through bodies.

The priests seated the corpse on the throne. The head tried to tilt back, and two attendants supported it invisibly. The king's face was not peaceful. The idea that the dead look peaceful is a courtesy invented by the living. His face held surprise. He looked like someone who had not expected to die.

Ian saw that expression.

Clearly.

The crown rose.

Outside, thousands of people bit the tips of their tongues at once. It was the pain of contract. Shorter and cleaner than the first collection. Nobles inside, bakers outside, children watching from roofs: all tasted blood and metal.

The crown touched the king's brow.

Krontium thorns entered the skin.

The corpse inhaled.

The sound was too small for anyone beyond the cathedral to hear, but the entire square stepped back. The city amplified the breath. Black walls accepted it, sent it up towers, and returned it. People heard the dead king's first breath inside their own ears.

Someone wept.

Someone knelt.

Someone shouted, “The eternal king.”

The king opened his eyes.

For a moment they were empty. Ian could not tell whether the emptiness belonged to the king or the metal. Then the king's lips moved. He tried to say his name. His mouth formed the first sound, and every candle in the cathedral bent low.

No voice came.

“The name would have been expensive,” Maret whispered.

“Did we lose the king's name?”

“No. The king's name is royal property. Such things are not surrendered easily.”

Then what had been lost?

The answer revealed itself after the ceremony. When the king took his first step, the crowd should have cheered. Instead, the square entered a strange pause. People studied one another's faces as if each had misplaced something.

Ian looked at the king.

The king was alive. Color had returned to his skin, his neck held straight, his fingers gripped the throne. Yet Ian suddenly could not say what he had seen moments before.

What expression had the king worn in death?

Surprise?

Anger?

Fear?

He tried to remember. He had seen it clearly. Before the crown touched him, the corpse's face had been more human than royal. It had said that even a king could fail to expect the end. Now the sentence had been torn from the page.

Ian looked around.

Maret had lost it too; the priest's eyes had tightened. The nobles were more confused. They had witnessed a miracle but could not remember what the miracle had covered. So they applauded more loudly. Applause is very useful for filling holes in memory.

That evening, the same question moved through the city.

Did His Majesty look peaceful?

No, he had not died.

But what did his face look like?

No one answered.

In the tax office cellar, the Krontium plate was already waiting. A new sentence glowed on its surface.

Second chapter. The face of death.

Ian did not touch it. The line entered him anyway. The face of death. The king's last humanity. The moment before power took hold of the body and made it useful again. The whole kingdom had lost it.

“They will grow used to it,” Maret said from behind him.

Ian was not surprised. Priests arrived a little earlier than necessary.

“Used to what?”

“The fact that the king does not die.”

“Or to not remembering that he did?”

Maret did not answer.

Water fell from the cellar ceiling. The new capital was said to be impossible to collapse, but rain had already found its way between stones. Ian watched the drop and felt an unreasonable relief. Not everything was perfect. Not yet.

“If the king dies again?”

“The crown will be placed again.”

“And each time we pay?”

“Each time the kingdom chooses the king.”

“The kingdom has never chosen.”

Maret finally looked directly at him.

“Choice is a name added afterward. What comes first is need.”

The sentence was wide enough to shelter every crime of the court. Ian asked nothing more. He covered the plate. Beneath the black cloth, Krontium continued to glow.

That night, the king entered the new capital.

Candles filled the windows to welcome the undying sovereign. Flower petals covered the streets. Soldiers raised spears. Children recited the royal name. The king lifted one hand from inside the carriage. His face had no expression. Not the expression of a living man. Not the expression of a dead one.

Ian stood at the back of the crowd.

As the carriage passed, a child beside him asked, “How long has he been king?”

The child's father laughed and opened his mouth to answer.

Nothing came.

The coronation had been today. But the king had been king before today. Before before. Always.

Krontium did not merely postpone death.

It shaved the edges from time.

Ian thought for the first time that if the city would not collapse, then people must be collapsing somewhere else.

The answer was too near.

Inside.
