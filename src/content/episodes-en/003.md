On the third morning, rain fell.

The Krontium city received rain strangely. Ordinary roofs shed water, but black metal held each drop for a moment. The drops brightened from within, gold at their centers, and if one watched too long, faces seemed to move inside them.

Citizens called it blessing.

<span class="memory-interference">Ian thought of digestion.</span>

The city was still swallowing the memories of the first and second days. Rain was what leaked from the process. He told no one. In the tax office, accurate metaphors were dangerous. Krontium did not distinguish them well, and the court distinguished jokes even worse.

He was sent to the north gate market.

The child from the first day was there.

Ian had not come looking for him. The third collection concerned the opening of the market, and Ian had been assigned to record it. Yet he recognized the boy at once. People who have lost waiting carry a particular hole in the face. The child did not run like the others. He stood among legs and baskets and looked toward the gate. Only someone who no longer knew whom he awaited could look at a doorway for that long.

His mother sold bread. Flour on her apron, hair tied back, a small burn on the back of one hand. Ian remembered that hand smoothing the child's hair on the first day. Now it cut loaves.

“You are from the tax office,” she said.

Her voice was polite, and beneath the politeness was exhaustion. Since the city's completion, people had learned a new way to look at tax officials. Before, tax was something one could complain about. Now tax vanished from inside the complaint.

“I am here to confirm the market record.”

“The record is posted there.”

She pointed with the bread knife. A royal proclamation hung at the market entrance. Rain did not wet it. It was not paper but a thin Krontium leaf, the letters floating gold on black.

The first market of the new capital proves the abundance of the kingdom.

Every merchant participating consents to the city's protection.

Consent caught in Ian's eye.

“Is the child well?”

He regretted the question at once. From a tax official, concern sounded like inspection.

The woman set down the knife.

“Does he look well?”

Ian looked at the boy. He stood under the arch, watching rain fall beyond it. The road outside led north toward the mines, and beyond the mines rose the mountains where the god's bones had been found. The child could not have known that.

“Since the first day, he watches doors,” she said. “If I ask whom he is waiting for, he says he does not know. But he says he must keep waiting. At night he wakes and opens the door. Rain makes it worse.”

<span class="memory-interference">The smell of waiting.</span>

Ian remembered the line on the plate. Krontium had taken a smell. But not only a smell. The smell had been the knot tying the waiting to a shape. When the knot loosened, the waiting drifted loose inside the child.

“Did you call a healer?”

“I called one. The healer said he was sensitive to blessing. The priest said he was well connected to the city. The tax office said it was a normal payment response.”

Her voice lowered.

“You have many names for things.”

Ian had none to offer.

The boy approached with the wooden horse in his hand, the same toy as before. One leg had broken. He did not seem to notice.

“Mister,” the boy said, “does the city remember people?”

Ian crouched to meet his eyes.

“Why do you ask?”

“The wall seems to know me.”

“Know what?”

“What I forgot.”

Rain thickened. Merchants dragged goods under awnings. Soldiers checked that proclamations remained fixed to the walls. Ian listened and felt the Krontium behind him. The wall made no sound, but silence could have weight.

“Do you know what you forgot?”

The boy shook his head.

“No. But I almost know.”

That was not a child's answer. Children usually say they do not know. Adults say they almost know.

His mother gripped his shoulder.

“Enough.”

The boy shrank slightly. Ian saw the woman's hand tremble. She was not afraid of what the boy said. She was afraid it might be true.

The market bell rang.

The third collection differed from the first two. There was no single grand event. The market opening, the first use of Krontium scales, the first exchange of new coin: priests called this normalization. The moment a miracle became routine, they said, was the moment a kingdom stabilized.

Ian hated that.

Routine changes people at the deepest level.

The first transaction was a loaf of black rye bread baked by the boy's mother. The buyer was a young man from the palace staff. He offered a coin, and she placed the loaf on the scale. The scale's central beam was Krontium. Priests said it weighed not matter but the balance of exchange.

The beam tilted.

A thin light rose from the metal.

The young man laughed.

The laugh did not belong. He was wet, late, and probably about to be scolded when he returned to the palace. Yet he laughed brightly, as if he had recovered something long lost.

Then the laugh went out.

He looked at the coin.

“Why did I buy this bread?”

“To eat it, I suppose,” the woman said.

“No. I was taking it to someone.”

He stood with the loaf in his hands. Rain fell from his hair. The market laughed at first. Forgetfulness, they said. Palace servants were always scattered. But the young man grew pale.

“Who was it?”

On the Krontium beam, a line appeared and vanished. Ian caught it.

The one waiting for breakfast.

He drew his portable record plate at once. It was smaller than his palm, and every use left his fingertips a little duller. Since the first day, full sensation had not returned.

“Third chapter,” he said quietly.

The plate did not respond.

“First market transaction.”

Nothing.

Maret would have known the correct wording. Priests always knew the sentences the metal liked. Ian hesitated and looked at the boy, still watching the gate.

“Residue of waiting.”

The plate heated.

A sentence rose.

Third chapter. The outline of a place to return.

When Ian read it, the market changed. Awnings, bread, wet stones, shoulders, spears: everything acquired a thin outline. People were not merely buying and selling. They were confirming that there was somewhere to go back to. Bread was an excuse for return. Coin was a promise that return remained possible.

Krontium ate the edge of that promise.

Seventeen people briefly lost their way in the market that day. They all knew their homes. Street names, door numbers, faces of family remained. But the sense that they ought to go home became faint. Their feet knew the route; their hearts did not.

The city did not collapse.

People lost directions inside themselves.

At dusk, Ian met the boy again beneath the north gate arch. The rain had stopped, and the sky held the blue reflection of Krontium towers.

“I think I know who I am waiting for,” the boy said.

Ian did not answer.

“Someone who has not been born yet.”

The words chilled him.

“Who told you that?”

“The wall.”

The boy pointed to the black pillar. Rain marks remained on its surface. Ian stepped close. At first the stains were accidental. Then, after he looked too long, they became almost letters.

The memories of the unborn are lightest.

Ian inhaled.

Krontium, the proclamations said, collected the memories of the living like tax. But if the metal could count future memory, then the kingdom's debt was not owed only by present citizens. Unborn children, unmade promises, loves not yet begun: the city held them as collateral.

Ian set a hand on the boy's shoulder.

“Did you tell your mother?”

The boy shook his head.

“She cries when I look at doors.”

That silenced Ian for a long time.

At night, in the tax office cellar, the Krontium plate received the third sentence completely. The outline of a place to return. Beneath it, smaller directions vanished into gold: the person waiting for breakfast, the height of a threshold, the habit of removing wet shoes, a cough from inside the house, the certainty that someone waits for me.

Ian stared at the last one.

The certainty that someone waits for me.

He tried to know whether he had ever possessed such certainty. His mother's kitchen. Scentless soup. A father who did not return. He remembered waiting, but the person at the end of waiting blurred.

Krontium had never taken only small things.

It chose doors that looked small.

Behind them were large rooms.

Ian did not cover the plate. Instead, for the first time, he took out blank paper. Real paper. Tearable, burnable, ruined by water. He began to write what he had seen.

The child watches doors.

The market loses the way home.

The metal calculates the future.

He hesitated before the final line.

The city does not collapse.

Therefore someone is collapsing in its place.

The paper trembled faintly beneath his hand. Everything not made of Krontium was weak. For the first time, Ian thought weak things might be necessary. Things that could tear. Things that could burn. Things that could be hidden before they were remembered by the wrong material.

That night, he removed a brick from the cellar wall and put the paper behind it.

The brick was not Krontium.

Not yet.
