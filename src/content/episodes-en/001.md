The city was completed at dawn.

The last pillar did not sink into the center of the temple, but into the marketplace beyond the north gate, a place that had not yet been given a name. People called it a pillar because they needed a word for it. Up close, it looked more like bone. It was black metal, and gold lines moved across its surface like veins. Whenever the smiths withdrew their hammers, those lines burned red for a breath and cooled again.

The metal was called Krontium.

The priests said it had been drawn from the bones of a god. No one asked which god. At first, everyone had known. On the first day of smelting, the miners sang the god's name as they dug into the mountain. On the second day, the smiths carved that name into the first line of the furnace prayer. On the third day, when the king himself tried to declare it, every mouth in the palace stopped.

No one remembered the name.

The kingdom did not call it an omen. It recorded the loss as the first miracle. If the metal made from a god's bones had taken the god's own name, then the metal was alive. And what lived could guard the kingdom.

After that, Krontium entered every important structure: the ribs of the walls, the cathedral ceiling, the prison doors, the customs scales, the back of the throne. It did not collapse. It did not rust. It did not bend. Fire heated it but could not persuade it to change shape. Bridges braced with Krontium survived floods. Towers ringed with Krontium kept their bells through earthquakes.

People began to believe that what could not fall must be good.

Ian did not believe it.

He worked at the north gate tax office. The name was old. The office no longer counted many coins, sacks of grain, or lengths of cloth. The capital had changed the shape of tax. Krontium did not ask for matter. Not gold, not silver, not wheat, not the blood of soldiers. The metal preferred quieter payments.

Memory.

The first collection was scheduled to begin with the city's completion ceremony. The royal court called it a blessing. Proclamations had been fixed to wells, bakeries, and bathhouse doors for three days: Every citizen shall participate in the permanence of the new capital. Participate was a useful word. People disliked compulsion, but they nodded at participation.

Ian reached the north gate square before the fourth bell. The crowd had not yet filled it. Fog arrived first. Whenever it touched the black metal, it tore into thin ribbons. Watching it, Ian had the sense that the city was breathing.

“You are not late.”

The voice came from behind him. Priest Maret crossed the stones, dragging a gray cloak through the damp. His face was smooth as cooled wax. Beneath his eyes lay the blue shadow of someone who had misplaced sleep.

“I thought the metal might take my name if I were late on the first day.”

Maret did not smile.

“Be careful with jokes. Krontium does not always distinguish metaphor.”

Ian did not ask whether that was a joke or a warning. Priests spoke that way. They loved less the things they knew than the fact that others did not know them.

A low table stood in the center of the square. On it lay the thin metal plate they called a ledger. Ian had always thought the name was wrong. A ledger could be closed. A book could be burned. This plate could do neither. Once Krontium accepted a sentence, the sentence remained.

He touched the surface.

It was cold. Somewhere very far away, someone seemed to call his name.

“What will it take today?”

Maret looked past him, toward the people entering the square: half-awake children, bakers with coats over their aprons, soldiers dragged from taverns, servants sent early to secure a view for noble households. They had come to witness the first morning of the new capital. They believed they would see history from the outside.

“Small things first,” Maret said.

“If the metal takes too much at once, they will fear the city. Today it will take smells, waiting, the outline of a face, the last measure of an old song. Things like that.”

“Most of them will not notice.”

“Most.”

That word unsettled Ian. Most always left someone outside it. Someone would notice. Someone would spend a life touching the missing place with the tongue of the mind. But no proclamation said that.

The sun rose, and the city lit.

Krontium did not reflect light. It accepted it, held it briefly, and returned it altered. Gold and cold blue traveled across black surfaces. The crowd gasped. Children reached out; adults caught their wrists. No one explained why.

The king's deputy climbed the platform. The king himself remained in the old capital. Until the new capital's name was announced, he would not set foot in it. Naming was a royal power, and power always looked more expensive when delayed.

The deputy read from a prepared page.

“Today we receive a city that will not fall.”

The crowd cheered.

“Today we bequeath walls, not ruins, to our children.”

The cheering grew.

“Today we give one memory and receive a thousand years of shelter.”

The square went quiet.

Ian saw their faces. Some had read the proclamations. Some had not. Some had heard of the tax but not understood it. Memory was too near to everyone, and therefore too vague. People knew how to be angry about houses, hands, debts, sickness. They were slower to be angry about memory.

Maret nodded.

Ian stood before the plate. As instructed, he placed his right hand on it and pressed his left hand to his chest. His heart was beating. For a moment he verified that it was his. Before Krontium, such doubts felt ordinary.

“Memory is fairer than blood.”

His voice moved through the square.

The sentence was not his. The temple had supplied it, the court had approved it, the tax office had made him repeat it each morning. Yet once spoken, it belonged to Ian. The crowd looked at him. If anyone suffered later, they would remember his face before the king's or the priests'.

The thought made him feel suddenly old.

The plate warmed beneath his palm. Then the warmth climbed his wrist, elbow, shoulder, and the back of his neck. The Krontium lines set into the square began to glow. They ran through roads, up walls, and into the towers. The whole city woke like a single nerve.

A child in the crowd stopped crying.

Ian saw him. Six years old, perhaps. One hand held his mother's hand; the other clutched a wooden horse. The crying had ended, but the face had not finished preparing to cry. The child opened his mouth and looked around.

“Why were you crying?”

His mother asked gently.

The child could not answer.

She smiled and smoothed his hair. A small thing, she thought. The child shook his toy, and she relaxed. But Ian saw how he kept looking beyond the north gate. The shape of the person he had waited for had vanished; only the direction of waiting remained.

A sentence rose on the metal plate.

First chapter. The smell of waiting.

It appeared less like writing than a wound. The Krontium surface split in a hair-thin line, and gold lifted through the cut. When Ian read it, he remembered his mother's kitchen: soup simmering late while she waited for his father to return. Ian had never liked the smell, but he knew it belonged to him.

Then the smell vanished.

He remembered the soup, the kitchen, the chair, his mother looking out the window. Only the smell was gone. The memory remained, but one door inside it had closed.

He wanted to lift his hand.

“Until it is finished,” Maret said.

It did not finish quickly. The plate combed through the crowd. An old woman lost the final note of a lullaby. A soldier lost the sound of a horse from his first battle. A baker lost the way his dead sister wrinkled her nose when she laughed. They paused, then moved on. The losses were too small to identify immediately.

The city ate the small things and shone.

By noon, the first collection ended. People went home praising the beauty of the city. They said the new capital truly looked impossible to destroy. Children laughed at their faces reflected in black walls. Nobles discussed the quality of the metal's sheen.

Ian carried the plate back to the tax office.

It was heavier than before.

That night, he could not sleep. From his room he could see the towers glowing faintly in the dark, as if the city were chewing through the memories it had taken. He lay in bed and recalled his mother's kitchen.

Soup.

Old chair.

Window.

Mother waiting.

The smell did not return.

Near dawn, he went down to the tax office cellar. The plate lay beneath a black cloth. The cellar was locked, but the first assessor had been given a key, and the key was Krontium too.

He lifted the cloth.

There was more than one sentence.

Under the first line were dozens of smaller entries: the smell of waiting, the last note of a lullaby, a sister's nose-wrinkled laugh, a horse's cry, the feel of winter gloves, the silence after a first lie, a mole on the back of a hand.

At the bottom, a new line formed in wet gold.

Tax assessor Ian Lavren.

Payment deferred.

Ian stopped breathing. He had thought he had paid nothing. But Krontium did not distinguish metaphor. He had stood as the one who collected, and so he was recorded as one who had not yet paid.

The city omitted no one.

Above him, the bell began to ring.

The first night was ending.
